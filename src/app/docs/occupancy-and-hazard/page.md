Correct hazard classification is the single most important decision in sprinkler system design. It determines the design density, the number of sprinklers in the design area, pipe sizes, water supply requirements, and ultimately whether the system will perform during a fire. Getting it wrong means the system is either underdesigned (dangerous) or overdesigned (unnecessarily expensive).

---

## NFPA 13 Occupancy Classifications

NFPA 13 divides occupancies into three main categories, each with subcategories. The classification is based on the quantity and combustibility of the contents and the expected heat release rate.

### Light Hazard

Light hazard occupancies contain low quantities of combustible materials with low heat release rates.

**Examples:**
- Churches and houses of worship
- Classrooms and educational facilities
- Hospital patient areas and corridors
- Libraries (reading and stack areas with 8-foot shelving or less)
- Museums
- Nursing homes and residential care facilities
- Offices
- Restaurant seating areas
- Theaters and auditoriums (excluding stages and backstage)
- Unused attics

**Typical design criteria:**
- Design area: 1,500 sq ft
- Common density: 0.10 gpm/sq ft (though the 2025 edition uses tables)
- Hose stream allowance: 100 gpm for 30 minutes

### Ordinary Hazard Group 1

Moderate quantity of combustibles with low combustibility. Storage heights do not exceed 8 feet.

**Examples:**
- Automobile parking garages
- Bakeries
- Beverage manufacturing
- Canneries
- Dairy products manufacturing
- Electronic plants
- Glass and glass products manufacturing
- Laundries
- Restaurant service areas

**Typical design criteria:**
- Design area: 1,500 sq ft
- Common density: 0.15 gpm/sq ft
- Hose stream allowance: 250 gpm for 60-90 minutes

### Ordinary Hazard Group 2

Moderate to high quantity of combustibles with moderate combustibility. Storage heights may be between 8 and 12 feet.

**Examples:**
- Cereal mills
- Chemical plants (ordinary)
- Cold storage warehouses
- Confectionery products
- Distilleries
- Dry cleaners
- Feed mills
- Horse stables
- Leather goods manufacturing
- Libraries (large stack areas)
- Machine shops
- Metalworking
- Paper and pulp mills
- Paper process plants
- Piers and wharves
- Post offices
- Printing and publishing
- Repair garages
- Textile manufacturing
- Tire manufacturing
- Tobacco products manufacturing
- Wood machining
- Wood product assembly

**Typical design criteria:**
- Design area: 1,500 sq ft
- Common density: 0.20 gpm/sq ft
- Hose stream allowance: 250 gpm for 60-90 minutes

### Extra Hazard Group 1

High quantity of combustibles, high heat release rates, or dust-producing operations. Little or no flammable or combustible liquids.

**Examples:**
- Aircraft hangars (maintenance)
- Die casting
- Metal extruding
- Plywood and particleboard manufacturing
- Printing (using inks with flashpoints below 100 degrees F)
- Rubber reclaiming, compounding, drying, milling, vulcanizing
- Sawmills
- Textile picking, opening, blending, garnetting, carding
- Upholstering with plastic foams

**Typical design criteria:**
- Design area: 2,500 sq ft
- Density range: 0.30 gpm/sq ft or higher
- Hose stream allowance: 500 gpm for 90-120 minutes

### Extra Hazard Group 2

Moderate to substantial quantities of flammable or combustible liquids, extensive combustible shielding, or rapid fire development.

**Examples:**
- Asphalt saturating
- Flammable liquid spraying
- Flow coating
- Manufactured home or modular building assembly
- Open oil quenching
- Plastic processing
- Solvent cleaning
- Varnish and lacquer dipping

**Typical design criteria:**
- Design area: 2,500 sq ft
- Density range: 0.35-0.40 gpm/sq ft or higher
- Hose stream allowance: 500 gpm for 90-120 minutes

## Commodity Classification for Storage

When the building is a storage occupancy (warehouse, distribution center, etc.), the occupancy classification alone is not sufficient. NFPA 13 requires a commodity classification to determine the specific sprinkler design criteria.

### The Three Factors

Commodity classification considers three elements together:

1. **The product itself** -- What is being stored (metal parts, electronics, plastics, paper goods)?
2. **The packaging** -- How is it packaged (shrink wrap, corrugated carton, wooden crate, exposed plastic)?
3. **The pallet type** -- Wood, plastic, or metal pallets

All three must be evaluated together. A metal product packed in expanded polystyrene and shrink-wrapped on a plastic pallet has a much higher commodity class than the same metal product on a wood pallet in a corrugated box.

### NFPA 13 Commodity Classes

**Class I** -- Noncombustible products on wood pallets, in single-layer corrugated cartons, with or without single-thickness dividers, or without packaging.

**Class II** -- Noncombustible products in wooden crates, multiple-thickness corrugated cartons, or similar combustible packing. Also includes Class I products stored in multiple layers of corrugated.

**Class III** -- Products made of wood, paper, natural fiber cloth, or Group C plastics, or products in ordinary corrugated cartons with a moderate amount of Group A or B plastics.

**Class IV** -- Products containing appreciable amounts of Group A plastics in packaging or product, or Class I through III products in Group A plastic packaging. Also includes free-flowing Group A plastics.

**High-Hazard Commodities** -- These go beyond Class IV and include:
- Group A plastics (expanded polystyrene, ABS, polyurethane foam, etc.)
- Rubber tires
- Rolled paper
- Flammable/combustible liquids
- Aerosols

### Plastic Groups

Understanding plastic groups is essential for commodity classification:

- **Group A** -- The most challenging plastics: ABS, acrylic, polycarbonate, polyethylene, polypropylene, polystyrene (especially expanded), polyurethane, PVC (highly plasticized), FRP
- **Group B** -- Moderate hazard plastics: cellulosics, fluoroplastics, nylon, silicone
- **Group C** -- Lowest hazard plastics: CPVC, melamine, phenolic, PVC (rigid or lightly plasticized), PVDC, urea formaldehyde

### Impact on Design

Moving up one commodity class can dramatically increase sprinkler requirements:

- Higher design densities or more sprinklers in the calculation
- Lower maximum storage heights without in-rack sprinklers
- Larger pipe sizes
- Greater water supply demands
- Potential need for in-rack sprinklers

## FM Global Approach

FM Global uses a different classification system from NFPA 13. Designers working on FM-insured properties must understand both.

### FM Global Hazard Categories

FM Global Data Sheet 3-26 uses three hazard categories:

- **Hazard Category 1 (HC-1)** -- Lightest sprinkler designs, similar to NFPA light/ordinary hazard
- **Hazard Category 2 (HC-2)** -- Moderate designs
- **Hazard Category 3 (HC-3)** -- Heaviest sprinkler designs, corresponding to higher hazard occupancies

### Key Differences from NFPA

- FM Global standards are generally **more conservative** (more stringent) than NFPA 13
- FM requirements are based on extensive fire testing at FM's research campus
- A difference in classification between NFPA and FM can mean very different pipe sizes, number of sprinklers, and water supply requirements
- FM Global reviews designs for compliance and adjusts insurance premiums based on the protection level provided
- The building owner must decide whether the increased cost of FM-level protection is justified by the insurance premium reduction

### FM Global Data Sheets

Key FM Global data sheets that affect sprinkler design include:

- **DS 2-0** -- Installation guidelines for automatic sprinklers
- **DS 3-26** -- Fire protection water demand for non-storage sprinklered properties
- **DS 8-9** -- Storage of Class 1, 2, 3, 4, and plastic commodities
- **DS 2-81** -- Inspection, testing, and maintenance (comparable to NFPA 25)
- **DS 7-29** -- Ignitable liquid storage
- **DS 8-1** -- Commodity classification

### Practical Implications

When a project involves FM Global insurance:

1. Determine the FM hazard category early in design
2. Compare FM requirements against NFPA 13 minimum code requirements
3. Design to the more stringent of the two
4. Submit designs to FM Global for review before installation
5. FM Global field engineers will inspect the installation for compliance

## Common Classification Challenges

### Mixed Occupancies

Many buildings contain multiple hazard levels. A single building might have light hazard offices, ordinary hazard manufacturing, and extra hazard spray booths. Each area must be classified and designed independently, with hydraulic calculations based on the most demanding area.

### Changing Commodity

One of the biggest real-world challenges is that storage facilities change their commodity mix over time. A warehouse designed for Class II paper goods may start receiving Class IV plastics-heavy products. The sprinkler system designed for the original commodity may be inadequate for the new one.

**Best practices:**
- Document the commodity classification assumed in the design
- Post signage noting maximum storage height and commodity class
- Require notification when commodity changes
- Include reasonable safety margins where practical

### AHJ Interpretation

Different AHJs may interpret the same occupancy differently. A medical office with a small procedure room might be classified as light hazard by one AHJ and ordinary hazard by another. Early communication with the AHJ is essential.

### Third-Party Consultants

Some projects engage third-party fire protection engineers or consultants to perform commodity classification. This is especially common for:

- Large warehouse and distribution projects
- Mixed-commodity storage facilities
- Facilities storing hazardous materials
- Projects where the commodity mix is complex or ambiguous

Having a professional fire protection engineer perform and document the classification provides a defensible basis for the sprinkler design and can help resolve disputes between the designer, owner, and AHJ.
