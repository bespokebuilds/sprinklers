The fire sprinkler design process transforms architectural and engineering plans into a complete, code-compliant fire protection system. This page outlines the workflow a designer follows from receiving a project through final approval.

---

## Design Workflow Overview

### Step 1: Project Intake and Review

**Gather documents:**
- Architectural plans (floor plans, reflected ceiling plans, building sections, details)
- Structural plans (framing plans, details -- needed for hanger attachment and seismic bracing)
- Mechanical plans (ductwork layout -- the primary routing conflict)
- Plumbing plans (domestic water connections, drain locations)
- Electrical plans (conduit and equipment locations)
- Specifications (Division 21 -- Fire Suppression, and related sections)
- Water supply data (flow test results)

**Initial assessment:**
- Building type and occupancy classification
- Construction type (combustible vs. noncombustible)
- Total square footage and number of stories
- Applicable codes and standards (NFPA 13, 13R, or 13D; local amendments)
- Special requirements (FM Global, seismic zone, historical preservation)
- System type (wet, dry, preaction, deluge, ESFR)

### Step 2: Hazard Classification

- Determine the occupancy hazard classification for each area of the building
- For storage occupancies, determine commodity classification
- Confirm with the AHJ if there is any ambiguity
- Document the classification basis -- it drives all subsequent design decisions

### Step 3: System Layout

- Select sprinkler head type, K-factor, temperature rating, and orientation for each area
- Lay out sprinkler heads on the plans per NFPA 13 spacing rules
- Route branch lines, cross mains, and feed mains
- Locate risers and connections to the water supply
- Design system drainage (main drain, auxiliary drains, test connections)
- Plan seismic bracing locations (if required)

### Step 4: Hydraulic Calculations

- Identify the hydraulically most remote area (check multiple candidates)
- Calculate flows and pressures through the system using Hazen-Williams
- Add hose stream allowance and elevation changes
- Compare system demand against available water supply
- Adjust pipe sizes or layout if the supply is insufficient
- Consider fire pump sizing if supply augmentation is needed

### Step 5: Shop Drawing Production

- Produce floor plan layouts showing all piping, heads, hangers, and devices
- Create riser diagrams
- Prepare detail drawings for complex assemblies
- Generate a bill of materials
- Prepare the hydraulic calculation report
- Prepare seismic calculation report (if applicable)

### Step 6: Submittal and Review

- Submit drawings and calculations to the AHJ for permit
- Submit to the general contractor and architect for coordination review
- Submit to the insurer (FM Global, etc.) if applicable
- Address review comments
- Obtain permit approval

### Step 7: Coordination

- Participate in BIM coordination (if applicable)
- Resolve routing conflicts with other trades
- Update drawings based on coordination results
- Resubmit if coordination changes affect previously approved elements

### Step 8: Support During Construction

- Answer RFIs from the field
- Process substitution requests
- Address field conditions that differ from drawings
- Prepare change order pricing for scope changes
- Produce as-built (record) drawings upon completion

## Key Designer Decisions

Throughout the design process, designers face recurring decision points:

### System Type Selection

Consider temperature, accidental discharge risk, fire growth potential, water supply capacity, and cost. See the System Types page for detailed guidance.

### Head Selection

Match the head to the application:
- Orientation (pendent, upright, sidewall, concealed)
- K-factor (based on hazard and available pressure)
- Temperature rating (based on ambient conditions)
- Response type (quick response vs. standard)
- Coverage area (standard vs. extended coverage)

### Pipe Material and Schedule

- Steel is the default for commercial and industrial
- Schedule 10 with grooved fittings for mains (cost savings)
- Schedule 40 with threaded fittings for branch lines 2 inches and smaller
- CPVC for light hazard where permitted

### Layout Approach

- Tree, loop, or grid based on building geometry and hydraulic efficiency
- Tree systems for simple rectangular buildings
- Loops for multi-story and irregular layouts
- Grids for large, open floor plans where pressure efficiency matters

## Design Documents Checklist

A complete design submittal package includes:

- [ ] Floor plan layouts (all floors and roof levels)
- [ ] Riser diagrams
- [ ] Detail drawings (valve assemblies, FDC, test connections, seismic bracing)
- [ ] Hydraulic calculations
- [ ] Seismic calculations (where required)
- [ ] Equipment cut sheets (heads, valves, alarm devices, pump)
- [ ] Bill of materials / stock list
- [ ] Design criteria summary (occupancy class, density, area, hose stream, water supply data)
- [ ] Site plan showing water supply connection and FDC location
- [ ] Specifications compliance matrix (if specified)

## Growing as a Designer

### For New Designers

- Study NFPA 13 systematically -- it is the foundation of everything
- Learn one software platform well (AutoSPRINK is the most marketable skill)
- Understand hydraulics before relying on software to calculate for you
- Visit job sites to see how your drawings become real installations
- Ask experienced designers about their design choices -- the "why" matters as much as the "how"
- Review AHJ comments on your drawings -- they are free education
- Join professional organizations (NFSA, AFSA, SFPE) for training and networking

### For Experienced Designers

- Stay current with code changes (NFPA 13 updates every 3 years)
- Learn specialty applications (storage, healthcare, high-rise)
- Understand FM Global requirements -- many clients require FM compliance
- Develop expertise in BIM and 3D coordination
- Mentor junior designers -- teaching deepens understanding
- Pursue NICET certification -- it is the industry credential for fire sprinkler designers
  - Level I: entry level
  - Level II: competent designer
  - Level III: senior designer, can stamp drawings in many jurisdictions
  - Level IV: expert level, system design and analysis
