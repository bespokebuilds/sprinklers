Fire sprinkler project management encompasses the full lifecycle of a project from contract award through closeout. The project manager (PM) is responsible for delivering the project on time, within budget, and to the required quality standards.

---

## Project Phases

### Phase 1: Pre-Construction

**Contract review:**
- Review contract documents (plans, specifications, general conditions, addenda)
- Identify scope, exclusions, and alternates
- Confirm schedule milestones and liquidated damages provisions
- Note special requirements (prevailing wage, minority participation, sustainability)

**Project setup:**
- Assign design team and field crew
- Establish project budget from the estimate
- Create a project schedule
- Set up job files and documentation systems
- Schedule kickoff meeting with the general contractor

**Submittals:**
- Prepare and submit product data for all major components
- Component submittals typically include:
  - Sprinkler heads (manufacturer, model, K-factor, temperature, finish)
  - Pipe (manufacturer, material, schedule, standard)
  - Valves (type, size, manufacturer, listing)
  - Alarm devices (flow switches, tamper switches, pressure switches)
  - Fire pump (if applicable -- type, capacity, driver, controller)
  - Hangers and supports
  - Backflow preventers
  - Seismic bracing components

### Phase 2: Design and Coordination

**System design:**
- Produce shop drawings (plans, riser diagrams, details, calculations)
- Submit to AHJ for permit
- Submit to general contractor for coordination review
- Submit to owner's insurer (FM Global, etc.) if required
- Address review comments and resubmit as needed

**BIM coordination:**
- Create 3D model of the sprinkler system
- Participate in coordination meetings with all trades
- Resolve clashes (physical conflicts between building systems)
- Update drawings based on coordination results

**Key coordination challenge:** The fire sprinkler designer often starts design after other trades have established their routing in the BIM model. Plumbing, mechanical, and electrical systems may already be designed, submitted, and sometimes approved by the time fire protection begins. Early engagement in the coordination process is critical to avoid being the last trade trying to fit into remaining space.

### Phase 3: Fabrication and Installation

**Fabrication management:**
- Release approved drawings for fabrication
- Monitor shop fabrication schedule
- Coordinate material deliveries to site
- Manage material procurement and lead times

**Field installation management:**
- Coordinate access and schedule with the general contractor
- Manage field crew (crew size, schedule, productivity)
- Conduct daily quality checks
- Resolve field conflicts and issue RFIs (Requests for Information)
- Track labor hours against budget
- Manage change orders for scope changes

### Phase 4: Testing and Inspection

**Pre-inspection preparation:**
- Complete all installation work
- Conduct internal quality review (punch list)
- Schedule hydrostatic test
- Prepare test documentation

**Testing:**
- Hydrostatic pressure test (200 psi for 2 hours, wet systems)
- Air test (40 psi for 24 hours, dry/preaction systems)
- Flush underground mains
- Test all alarm and supervisory devices
- Flow test fire pump (if applicable)

**AHJ inspection:**
- Schedule inspection with the authority having jurisdiction
- Have all required documents on site
- Walk the system with the inspector
- Address any deficiencies and schedule re-inspection if needed

### Phase 5: Closeout

**Required closeout documents:**

| Document | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| Contractor's Material and Test Certificate | Certifies materials and test results per NFPA 13 |
| As-built (record) drawings | Document the system as actually installed |
| Hydraulic calculations | Final calculations reflecting as-built conditions |
| Operation and maintenance manuals | Instructions for the building owner/operator |
| Valve chart | Location and identification of all system valves |
| Spare head cabinet | Stocked with required spare heads and wrench |
| Owner training | Instruct building personnel on system operation |
| Warranty documentation | Contractor warranty, manufacturer warranties |
| Lien releases | Proof of payment to subcontractors and suppliers |
| Permit sign-off | AHJ final approval |

## BIM and CAD in Fire Protection

### BIM Requirements

BIM (Building Information Modeling) is increasingly required on commercial and institutional projects.

**What the fire protection contractor contributes to BIM:**
- 3D model of all sprinkler piping, heads, hangers, and equipment
- LOD (Level of Detail/Development) appropriate to the project phase
- Coordination-ready models exported in compatible formats (IFC, RVT, DWG)

**Software used:**
- **AutoSPRINK** with 3D modeling capabilities
- **Revit** with fire protection content and plugins
- **SprinkCAD** for cloud-based modeling
- **Navisworks** or **BIM 360** for clash detection and coordination
- **AutoCAD** for 2D documentation

**BIM coordination process:**
1. Each trade creates their 3D model
2. Models are combined (federated) in coordination software
3. Automated clash detection identifies conflicts
4. Coordination meetings resolve conflicts (typically weekly during active design)
5. Models are updated and re-checked
6. Process repeats until clashes are resolved

### CAD Standards

Fire protection drawings typically follow:

- National CAD standards (NCS) for layer naming and organization
- Company-specific standards for symbols, line types, and text
- Project-specific requirements from the architect or general contractor
- AHJ-specific requirements for drawing content and format

## Schedule Management

### Critical Path Considerations

Fire sprinkler installation is typically on or near the critical path because:

- Rough-in must occur after structural framing but before ceilings close
- Hydrostatic testing must occur before ceilings and finishes
- Head trim-out must occur after ceiling installation
- AHJ inspection must occur before certificate of occupancy

### Typical Durations

| Activity | Typical Duration |
|----------|-----------------|
| Design and drawing production | 2-6 weeks |
| AHJ permit review | 2-4 weeks (varies widely by jurisdiction) |
| Coordination/BIM | 2-8 weeks (overlaps with permit review) |
| Material procurement | 1-4 weeks (standard items); 6-12 weeks (fire pumps, specialty items) |
| Shop fabrication | 1-3 weeks |
| Field rough-in | Varies by building size (2-12+ weeks) |
| Hydrostatic testing | 1-2 days |
| Ceiling trim-out | 1-4 weeks |
| AHJ final inspection | 1-2 days (plus time to correct deficiencies) |

### Schedule Risks

- **Permit delays** -- some jurisdictions take weeks or months for plan review
- **Design changes** -- architectural or structural changes late in the project require sprinkler redesign
- **Material lead times** -- specialty items (ESFR heads, fire pumps, specialty valves) may have long lead times
- **Trade coordination delays** -- other trades not completing their work on schedule pushes sprinkler installation
- **Inspection failures** -- deficiencies found during AHJ inspection require correction and re-inspection

## Change Order Management

Changes are inevitable on construction projects. Effective change management protects both the contractor and the project.

### Common Change Triggers

- Architectural or structural design changes
- Owner-requested scope additions
- Field conditions differing from drawings
- Coordination conflicts requiring re-routing
- Code interpretation changes
- Hazard classification changes (commodity change in warehouses)

### Change Order Process

1. **Identify** the change and document it
2. **Price** the change (material, labor, overhead, profit)
3. **Submit** the change order request with backup documentation
4. **Negotiate** with the general contractor or owner
5. **Execute** the approved change
6. **Track** the cost against the approved change order amount

### Pricing Changes

Change order pricing typically includes:

- Direct material cost (with verification quotes)
- Direct labor (hours x burden rate)
- Overhead markup (typically 10-15% on direct costs)
- Profit markup (typically 10-15% on total)
- Subcontractor markup (if applicable, typically 10%)

## Quality Management

### Internal Quality Control

Good fire protection contractors maintain quality through:

- **Design review** -- a second designer reviews drawings and calculations before submission
- **Fabrication inspection** -- quality checks at the shop before shipping
- **Field supervision** -- foremen inspect work daily
- **Pre-inspection punch list** -- the PM or superintendent walks the job before calling for AHJ inspection
- **Photo documentation** -- photographs of key installations before they are concealed

### Common Quality Issues

The most frequently cited deficiencies during AHJ inspections:

1. Sprinkler heads painted over
2. Missing escutcheons
3. Obstructions blocking sprinkler spray patterns
4. Inadequate clearance between sprinklers and storage
5. Missing or incorrect signage
6. Hanger spacing exceeds maximums
7. Wrong head type, K-factor, or temperature rating installed
8. Spare head cabinet missing, empty, or stocked with wrong heads
9. Missing or inoperable inspector's test connection
10. Alarm and supervisory devices not properly connected or labeled

## Communication and Reporting

### Regular Reporting

The PM should provide regular updates to stakeholders:

- **Weekly**: Progress update to the general contractor (percentage complete, upcoming activities, issues)
- **Monthly**: Financial report to company management (cost to date vs. budget, projected final cost)
- **As needed**: RFI submissions, change order requests, schedule updates

### Documentation

Maintain thorough records throughout the project:

- Daily field reports (crew size, hours worked, work accomplished, issues)
- Correspondence log (emails, letters, meeting notes)
- RFI log (requests for information, with status tracking)
- Change order log (with status and financial tracking)
- Submittal log (with approval status)
- Inspection reports
- Test reports and certificates
- Photo documentation

### Key Relationships

The fire protection PM interacts with:

- **General contractor** -- primary point of contact for schedule, coordination, and payment
- **Architect** -- for design questions and ceiling coordination
- **Structural engineer** -- for hanger loads and seismic bracing attachment
- **Mechanical engineer** -- for fire protection design parameters (if the system was specified by an engineer rather than designed by the contractor)
- **Fire alarm contractor** -- for monitoring device wiring and programming
- **AHJ** -- for permit review, inspections, and code interpretation
- **Owner** -- for operational requirements and system training
- **Insurer** -- for FM Global or other insurance carrier review and approval
