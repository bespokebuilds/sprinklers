Field installation is where the design and fabrication come together. Proper installation practices ensure the system performs as designed, passes inspection, and provides reliable fire protection for the life of the building.

---

## Installation Sequence

A typical fire sprinkler installation follows this general sequence:

### 1. Underground Rough-In

Before the building slab is poured:

- Install underground supply mains from the water source to the building
- Install underground piping within the building footprint (under-slab mains)
- Stub up risers through slab sleeves
- Hydrostatic test underground piping
- Flush underground mains to clear debris

### 2. Rough-In (Above Ground)

After the structural frame is up but before ceilings and walls are closed:

- Install risers and riser trim (valves, alarm devices, drain connections)
- Hang main lines (feed mains, cross mains)
- Install branch lines
- Install hangers and supports
- Install seismic bracing (in seismic zones)
- Coordinate with other trades for routing conflicts

### 3. Trim-Out (Finish Work)

After ceilings are installed:

- Install sprinkler heads (drop heads through ceiling tiles or connect to concealed-head housings)
- Install escutcheons and cover plates
- Install spare head cabinet
- Complete signage (valve identification, riser signage)
- Final adjustments to head positions

### 4. Testing and Commissioning

Before the system goes into service:

- Hydrostatic pressure test
- Flushing
- Flow test (if fire pump is involved)
- Alarm and supervisory device testing
- AHJ inspection
- Commissioning and system turn-on

## Pipe Hanging and Support

### Hanger Types

- **Clevis hangers** -- most common for horizontal pipe, wraps around the pipe with a bolt connection to the structure above
- **Ring hangers** -- encircle the pipe, attached with a rod to the structure
- **Trapeze hangers** -- support multiple parallel pipes on a horizontal channel
- **U-bolts** -- simple and economical for smaller pipe
- **Beam clamps** -- attach hangers to structural steel beams
- **Concrete inserts** -- pre-installed in concrete for rod attachment
- **Post-installed anchors** -- drilled and installed in existing concrete

### Hanger Spacing

NFPA 13 specifies maximum hanger spacing based on pipe size:

| Pipe Size | Maximum Hanger Spacing |
|-----------|----------------------|
| 1" | 12 feet |
| 1-1/4" | 12 feet |
| 1-1/2" | 15 feet |
| 2" | 15 feet |
| 2-1/2" | 15 feet |
| 3" and larger | 15 feet |
| CPVC (all sizes) | Per manufacturer listing |

Additional hangers are required:

- Within 12 inches of each change of direction
- On each side of a flexible coupling
- At the end of a branch line (within 6 feet of the last head)
- To prevent pipe from being overstressed at any point

### Load Calculations

Hangers must support the weight of:

- The pipe itself (filled with water for wet systems)
- Fittings and valves
- A 250-pound point load applied at any point between hangers (NFPA 13 requirement)
- Seismic loads (in seismic zones)

## Testing Requirements

### Hydrostatic Test

The hydrostatic test verifies the integrity of all piping joints and connections.

**Wet systems:**
- Test pressure: **200 psi** for 2 hours
- Or 50 psi above static pressure, whichever is greater
- No visible leaks permitted
- Test must include the FDC piping
- Gauge must hold steady (allowance for minor temperature-related pressure changes)

**Dry and preaction systems:**
- Hydrostatic test: **200 psi** for 2 hours
- Pneumatic (air) test: **40 psi** for 24 hours with less than **1.5 psi** pressure loss
- Both tests must pass

### Flushing

All underground supply piping must be flushed before connection to the sprinkler system to remove construction debris, dirt, and foreign material.

**Minimum flush flow rates by pipe size:**

| Pipe Size | Minimum Flush Flow (gpm) |
|-----------|------------------------|
| 4" | 390 |
| 6" | 880 |
| 8" | 1,560 |
| 10" | 2,440 |
| 12" | 3,520 |

**Flush requirements:**
- Flush until water runs clear with no signs of foreign material
- Measure flow using a pitot tube or other acceptable means
- Document the flush results

### Alarm and Supervisory Device Testing

Before acceptance:

- Test every waterflow switch for proper alarm initiation (within 90 seconds per NFPA 72)
- Test every tamper switch for proper supervisory signal
- Verify alarm transmission to the central monitoring station
- Test the water motor gong (mechanical alarm)
- Verify all signals are correctly identified at the fire alarm panel

## Acceptance Inspection

The AHJ will conduct an acceptance inspection before the system is placed in service. The contractor must have the following ready:

### Documents Required

- **Approved shop drawings** (stamped by the AHJ)
- **Hydraulic calculations**
- **Contractor's Material and Test Certificate for Aboveground Piping** (NFPA 13 form)
- **Underground piping certificates** (material, testing, flushing)
- **Product data sheets** for all major components
- **Cut sheets** for sprinkler heads, valves, alarm devices
- **Seismic calculations** (where applicable)
- **Fire pump test results** (if applicable)

### Inspection Points

The AHJ inspector typically checks:

- Sprinkler head spacing and clearance from obstructions
- Hanger spacing and type
- Pipe size and material (spot checks against drawings)
- Valve accessibility and identification
- System signage
- Spare head cabinet (stocked with correct quantity and type of spare heads and wrench)
- FDC location, signage, and accessibility
- Drain and test connection locations
- Hydrostatic test results documentation
- Alarm device functionality

## Coordination with Other Trades

Fire sprinkler installation must be coordinated with every other building system. Poor coordination leads to costly rework, delays, and failed inspections.

### Common Coordination Points

| Trade | Coordination Issue |
|-------|-------------------|
| **Structural** | Beam penetrations, seismic brace attachment, slab sleeves |
| **Mechanical (HVAC)** | Ductwork routing conflicts, clearance above ceilings |
| **Electrical** | Conduit and cable tray conflicts, lighting fixture proximity |
| **Plumbing** | Domestic water connections, shared risers, drain coordination |
| **Ceiling contractor** | Head locations, tile layout, access panels |
| **Fire alarm** | Device wiring, panel connections, signal programming |
| **General contractor** | Schedule, access, wall/ceiling closure dates |

### BIM Coordination

On larger projects, BIM (Building Information Modeling) coordination is standard practice:

- All trades create 3D models of their systems
- Models are combined to detect **clashes** (physical conflicts between systems)
- Coordination meetings resolve clashes before installation begins
- The fire sprinkler designer typically starts with the most flexibility because the system is not yet engineered when other trades begin

**Key challenge:** Plumbing, electrical, and HVAC contractors often come to BIM coordination with their systems already engineered, submitted, and sometimes approved. The fire sprinkler designer frequently starts with nothing and must produce design drawings that fit around systems already established in the model. This puts the fire protection trade at a disadvantage in coordination, and early engagement helps mitigate this.

## Common Installation Issues

1. **Sprinkler heads painted over** -- Any head that has been painted must be replaced. Painted heads may not activate properly. This is the single most common violation found during inspections.

2. **Inadequate clearance from storage** -- The 18-inch clearance below sprinklers must be maintained. This is an ongoing operational requirement, not just an installation requirement.

3. **Missing escutcheons** -- Every head that penetrates a ceiling must have an escutcheon (trim ring) that covers the annular space around the pipe.

4. **Hangers attached to other systems** -- Sprinkler hangers must be attached to the building structure, not to ductwork, conduit, ceiling grid, or other piping systems.

5. **Wrong head type installed** -- Heads must match the shop drawings. Wrong K-factor, temperature, orientation, or model can void the system listing.

6. **Obstructions added after inspection** -- New obstructions (lighting, signage, ductwork) installed after the sprinkler system is approved can compromise sprinkler performance.

7. **Missing or empty spare head cabinet** -- NFPA 13 requires a minimum number of spare heads (6 for systems with fewer than 300 heads, up to 24 for very large systems) plus a wrench.

## Safety on Site

### Working at Heights

Most sprinkler installation involves working at heights:

- Scissor lifts and boom lifts for high-ceiling work
- Scaffolding for sustained work in one area
- Ladders for finish work (trim-out)
- Fall protection requirements per OSHA

### Hot Work

When welding or brazing is required:

- Hot work permit from the general contractor
- Fire watch during and 30-60 minutes after work
- Remove or protect combustible materials within 35 feet
- Fire extinguisher immediately available

### Confined Spaces

Entry into valve pits, underground vaults, and equipment rooms may require confined space entry procedures per OSHA.
