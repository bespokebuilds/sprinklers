Fabrication is the process of cutting, preparing, and assembling pipe and fittings into the components that make up a fire sprinkler system. Modern fire protection contractors fabricate much of the system in a controlled shop environment before shipping pre-assembled pieces to the job site for installation.

---

## Shop Drawings

Before any fabrication begins, detailed shop drawings (also called installation drawings) must be produced. These drawings serve as the blueprint for both fabrication and field installation.

### What Shop Drawings Include

- **Piping layout** -- plan views of all sprinkler piping, typically one drawing per floor or zone
- **Riser diagrams** -- vertical sections showing risers, floor connections, and equipment
- **Hydraulic calculations** -- proof that the system meets design criteria
- **Bill of materials** -- complete list of all pipe, fittings, heads, hangers, and accessories
- **Fabrication details** -- isometric or detail drawings of complex assemblies
- **Seismic calculations and brace locations** (where applicable)
- **Head schedules** -- listing every sprinkler with type, K-factor, temperature rating, and finish
- **Hanger and support details**

### Approval Process

1. Designer produces shop drawings from architectural, structural, and MEP plans
2. Drawings submitted to the **AHJ** (fire marshal, building department) for permit review
3. Drawings submitted to the **general contractor** for coordination review
4. Drawings submitted to the **owner's insurance carrier** (FM Global, etc.) if applicable
5. Review comments addressed and resubmitted as needed
6. Approved drawings released for fabrication

### Drawing Software

Common design and drawing software includes:

- **AutoSPRINK** -- dominant industry software combining drafting with hydraulic calculations
- **SprinkCAD** -- cloud-based platform
- **Revit** (with fire protection plugins) -- for BIM-integrated projects
- **AutoCAD** -- general drafting, sometimes with custom fire protection tools
- **HydraCAD** -- design and calculation tool

## Prefabrication

### Shop Fabrication Process

Modern fire protection contractors fabricate as much as possible in the shop:

1. **Material receipt and inspection** -- verify pipe, fittings, and components against the bill of materials
2. **Pipe cutting** -- cut to lengths specified on shop drawings using band saws or cold-cut saws
3. **End preparation** -- thread, groove, or bevel pipe ends depending on the joining method
4. **Assembly** -- for complex sub-assemblies, components may be partially assembled (e.g., riser manifolds, valve assemblies)
5. **Labeling** -- each piece or assembly is labeled with a piece number matching the shop drawings
6. **Loading** -- pieces are loaded in installation sequence for efficient field installation

### Benefits of Prefabrication

- **Quality control** -- shop conditions allow better quality than field work
- **Speed** -- shop workers can fabricate much faster than field workers
- **Reduced field labor** -- significant portions of the system arrive ready to hang
- **Less site congestion** -- fewer workers and less equipment needed on site
- **Reduced waste** -- drops and cutoffs can be reused more efficiently
- **Weather independence** -- fabrication continues regardless of site conditions

### What Gets Fabricated in Shop vs. Field

| Shop Fabrication | Field Work |
|-----------------|------------|
| Pipe cutting to length | Hanging and supporting pipe |
| Threading and grooving | Making final connections |
| Riser assemblies | Installing sprinkler heads |
| Valve manifold assemblies | Routing pipe through building |
| Complex branch line assemblies | Adjusting for field conditions |
| Test and drain assemblies | Connecting to water supply |

## Joining Methods

### Threading

Threading cuts helical grooves into the pipe end that mate with threaded fittings to create a mechanical joint sealed with thread sealant (Teflon tape or pipe dope).

**Equipment required:**
- Threading machine with cutting dies (manual or powered)
- Pipe vise or holding fixture
- Reamer (to remove internal burr after threading)
- Thread sealant

**Where used:**
- Pipe sizes **2 inches and smaller** (most common)
- Can be used on larger sizes with Schedule 40 pipe
- Branch line connections to sprinkler heads (virtually always threaded at the head)

**Standards:**
- NPT (National Pipe Thread) taper threads per ANSI/ASME B1.20.1
- NFPA 13 requires Schedule 40 (or Schedule 30 for 8" and larger) when using threaded connections

**Best practices:**
- Always ream the pipe after threading to remove the internal burr
- Apply thread sealant to the male threads only
- Engage threads fully -- minimum thread engagement per NFPA 13
- Inspect for proper thread depth and profile

### Grooved

Grooved joining is the dominant method for fire sprinkler mains and larger branch lines.

**Types of grooves:**
- **Roll groove** -- the pipe end is cold-formed between rollers to create an indentation around the circumference. Faster and preferred for Schedule 10 pipe
- **Cut groove** -- a lathe-type cutter removes material to create the groove. Required for some heavy-wall pipe and fittings

**Equipment required:**
- Roll grooving machine (portable or bench-mounted)
- Or cut grooving machine
- Groove depth gauge for verification
- Lubricant for grooving operation

**Where used:**
- Pipe sizes **2 inches and larger** (most common)
- Can be used on smaller sizes
- All system types (wet, dry, preaction, deluge)

**Key advantages for fire protection:**
- Allows use of Schedule 10 pipe (significant cost savings)
- No hot work required (important in occupied buildings)
- Fast assembly and disassembly
- Flexible couplings accommodate building movement and thermal expansion
- Listed for up to 300 psi working pressure

### Welding

Welding creates a permanent joint by fusing pipe and fittings together at high temperature.

**Where used in fire protection:**
- Large-diameter mains (6 inches and above) in some applications
- Outlet connections (welded-on fittings for branch takeoffs)
- Shop fabrication of spool pieces
- Less common than grooved in modern fire sprinkler work

**Requirements:**
- Welders must be certified per AWS (American Welding Society) standards
- AWS D10.9 specifically covers welding of fire sprinkler piping
- Hot work permits required for all welding on construction sites
- Welds subject to inspection per NFPA 13
- Post-weld cleaning of pipe interior to remove slag and debris

**Considerations:**
- Welded joints are permanent -- modifications require cutting and re-welding
- Fire watch required during and after welding operations
- Not practical for most field installation due to hot work restrictions
- Creates the strongest joint but at higher labor cost

### Press-Connect and Other Mechanical Methods

Some newer joining methods are entering the fire protection market:

- **Press-connect fittings** -- use a hydraulic tool to crimp a fitting onto the pipe. Growing in popularity for copper and some steel applications
- **Push-fit fittings** -- used in some CPVC residential applications

## Quality Control

### Pre-Installation Checks

Before any fabricated piece leaves the shop or is installed:

- Verify pipe sizes match shop drawings
- Confirm all threads are clean, properly cut, and properly sealed
- Verify grooves are the correct depth and width per manufacturer specifications
- Ensure all fittings are listed (UL/FM) and correct for the application
- Confirm gaskets are the correct type (EPDM for water, special compounds for other fluids)
- Label verification -- piece numbers match drawings

### Common Fabrication Errors

1. **Wrong pipe schedule** -- mixing up Schedule 10 and Schedule 40 pieces
2. **Incorrect groove depth** -- too shallow causes leaks, too deep weakens the pipe
3. **Missing reaming after threading** -- the internal burr restricts flow and collects debris
4. **Wrong fitting orientation** -- tees, crosses, and reducing fittings installed in wrong direction
5. **Unlisted components** -- using hardware-store fittings instead of listed fire protection fittings
6. **Incorrect pipe lengths** -- errors in measurement that require field correction

## Labor Considerations

### Shop Labor Rates

Shop fabrication labor is generally less expensive than field labor because:

- Workers are more productive in a controlled environment
- Equipment is permanently installed and readily available
- No travel time or site mobilization costs
- Weather is not a factor

Typical shop labor rates vary by region but are generally 15-30% lower than equivalent field labor rates.

### Field Labor Rates

Field installation labor rates for fire sprinkler fitters typically range from **$50 to $150 per hour** including burden (benefits, taxes, insurance), depending on:

- Geographic location (union vs. non-union, cost of living)
- Prevailing wage requirements (government projects)
- Working conditions (heights, confined spaces, occupied buildings)
- Shift premiums (overtime, night work)

### Labor Productivity

Rough rules of thumb for field installation:

- **Pendant/upright heads**: 1.0 to 1.25 man-hours per head (including rough-in and trim)
- **Warehouse uprights**: 15 to 20 minutes per head
- **A 2-person crew** can rough in approximately 40 heads per shift in open warehouse conditions
- Mains and cross mains: varies widely by pipe size, height, and building complexity
