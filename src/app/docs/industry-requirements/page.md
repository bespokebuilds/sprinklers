Different industries and building types present unique challenges for fire sprinkler design. This page covers the specific requirements, standards, and practical considerations that apply to the most commonly encountered occupancies.

---

## Warehousing and Storage

Storage occupancies are among the most demanding and complex sprinkler design projects. The fire load, storage height, and commodity type drive significantly higher water demands than non-storage occupancies.

### Key Design Factors

- **Commodity classification** (Class I through IV, plus Group A plastics and other high-hazard commodities)
- **Storage height** -- high-piled storage is defined as anything over 12 feet
- **Storage arrangement** -- solid-piled, palletized, rack storage, bin-box, shelf storage
- **Aisle width** -- affects fire behavior and sprinkler performance
- **Clearance to ceiling** -- sprinklers are most effective 1.5 to 4.5 feet above storage height

### System Selection for Storage

| Approach | Best For | Key Trade-offs |
|----------|----------|----------------|
| ESFR | High-piled storage up to 40 ft; eliminates in-rack sprinklers | Requires strong water supply (50-100+ psi at remote head) |
| CMSA | Storage where ESFR water demands are too high | Lower pressure but more storage configuration limits |
| CMDA with in-rack | Flexible commodity storage, changing configurations | More piping (in-rack lines) but adapts to changes |

### In-Rack Sprinklers

When ceiling-only protection is insufficient, in-rack sprinklers are installed within the storage racks at intermediate levels. These:

- Reduce the required ceiling sprinkler density
- Are necessary for most rack storage above certain heights depending on commodity
- Add significant cost and complexity (piping runs through rack aisles)
- Must be coordinated with rack layout and protected from forklift damage
- Require barriers between racks to prevent fire spread through flue spaces

## Healthcare Facilities

Healthcare occupancies require full sprinkler protection per NFPA 101 (Life Safety Code) and NFPA 99 (Health Care Facilities Code).

### Standards That Apply

- **NFPA 13** -- system design and installation
- **NFPA 101** -- life safety requirements specific to healthcare
- **NFPA 99** -- health care facilities code
- **NFPA 25** -- inspection, testing, and maintenance
- **Joint Commission** standards for accredited facilities

### Key Requirements

- **Wet pipe systems** are the preferred and most commonly used type in healthcare facilities
- **Quick-response sprinklers** are required or strongly recommended in patient care areas
- **Full coverage** is required in all patient care areas, kitchens, mechanical rooms, and storage
- Sprinklers may be **omitted from small closets** (6 sq ft or less) in patient rooms if the room sprinkler can cover the closet within NFPA 13 distance requirements
- **Electrical supervision** is required for any condition that could impair the system
- System outages exceeding **4 hours in 24 hours** require either evacuation or fire watch

### Practical Considerations

- Infection control requirements may limit access for installation and maintenance
- Ceiling types (hard ceiling, lay-in tile, interstitial space) affect head type selection
- Concealed and institutional heads are common for aesthetics and tamper resistance
- Coordination with medical gas systems, nurse call systems, and patient monitoring is critical
- Areas with MRI equipment have special requirements (non-ferrous components)

## High-Rise Buildings

A high-rise building is typically defined as a building with an occupied floor more than 75 feet above the lowest level of fire department access.

### Sprinkler and Standpipe Requirements

- **Full automatic sprinkler protection** is required throughout
- **Class I standpipe system** is required per NFPA 14
- Standpipes must be automatic or semi-automatic
- Sprinkler systems allow Class I standpipes where Class III would otherwise be required

### Standpipe Design

- Hydraulically remote standpipe must flow **250 gpm** at the highest outlet and an additional **250 gpm** from the next highest outlet
- Each additional standpipe requires **250 gpm** up to a maximum of **1,000 gpm** (for fully sprinklered buildings)
- Hose connections at each main floor landing of required exit stairs
- Connection heights between **3 and 5 feet** above the floor

### Zoning and Pressure

- Very tall buildings may require **zone pumps** to manage pressure differentials between upper and lower floors
- Pressure-reducing valves may be needed at lower-floor hose connections to prevent excessive pressures
- Fire department connections (FDCs) must be provided for each zone

### Water Supply

- Fire pump(s) are almost always required
- Dual water supplies or on-site storage tanks are common
- Backup power for fire pumps (diesel or generator) is typically required

## Residential Systems

Residential sprinkler systems are designed under different standards depending on building type.

### NFPA 13D -- One- and Two-Family Dwellings

- Designed for **life safety** (to provide escape time), not property protection
- Maximum water supply: **40 gpm** for one or two sprinklers operating for **10 minutes**
- Sprinklers required only in **living areas** (bedrooms, living rooms, kitchens, hallways)
- **Exempt areas**: small bathrooms, closets, garages, carports, attics, concealed non-living spaces
- Does not require listed fire pumps or tanks (NFPA 20 and 22 not referenced)
- **Multipurpose systems** permitted (sprinkler piping also serves domestic plumbing fixtures)
- **Stand-alone systems** also permitted (dedicated sprinkler piping separate from domestic plumbing)
- CPVC and PEX piping are commonly used
- Cost: typically $1 to $2 per square foot in new construction

### NFPA 13R -- Residential up to Four Stories

- Applies to residential occupancies up to **four stories and 60 feet** in height
- More comprehensive than 13D but less than full NFPA 13
- Water supply requirements similar to NFPA 13 and NFPA 13R require fire pumps per NFPA 20 and tanks per NFPA 22 when used
- Sprinkler coverage is more extensive than 13D but may still exclude certain low-risk spaces

### NFPA 13 -- Residential in Larger Buildings

- Full NFPA 13 applies to residential occupancies in buildings over four stories or 60 feet
- Listed residential sprinklers must be used in dwelling units
- All areas protected including those exempt under 13R

## Data Centers

Data centers represent a unique challenge: the fire protection system must protect against fire while minimizing the risk of accidental water damage to extremely sensitive equipment.

### System Selection

**Preaction systems** are the standard for data center environments:

- **Single interlock** is most common -- detection system activation opens the valve; sprinkler activation discharges water
- **Double interlock** provides additional protection against accidental discharge
- The detection system (typically VESDA or spot-type smoke detectors) must be designed and maintained to high standards

### Design Considerations

- Coordinate with **underfloor cable tray** layout for sprinkler coverage below raised floors
- Above-ceiling spaces may also require sprinklers
- **Clean agent suppression** systems (FM-200, Novec 1230) are sometimes used in addition to or instead of sprinklers for equipment-level protection
- Drainage planning is critical -- a single sprinkler activation can release hundreds of gallons before suppression
- **Very Early Smoke Detection Apparatus (VESDA)** systems provide earlier warning than conventional detectors

## Parking Garages

### Classification and System Type

- Classified as **Ordinary Hazard Group 1** per NFPA 13
- **Dry pipe systems** are common in unheated or open parking structures
- Some garages use wet systems with heat-traced piping in cold climates

### Design Considerations

- Sprinklers must be protected from vehicle impact in low-clearance areas
- Head guards may be required
- Upright heads are common to avoid damage to pendant heads from vehicles
- Sloped parking decks require attention to drainage and pitch
- ESFR and CMSA are generally not used in parking garages

## Aircraft Hangars

Aircraft hangars are among the most demanding fire protection applications due to large quantities of aviation fuel and combustible materials.

### Applicable Standards

- **NFPA 409**: Standard on Aircraft Hangars
- **NFPA 13**: Chapter 22 covers sprinkler requirements influenced by NFPA 409

### System Types by Hangar Group

| Hangar Group | Description | Typical System |
|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| Group I | Major repair, over 40,000 sq ft | Foam-water deluge + overhead sprinklers |
| Group II | Major repair, under 40,000 sq ft | Foam-water + overhead sprinklers |
| Group III | Service/storage, limited operations | Overhead sprinklers per NFPA 13 |
| Group IV | Membrane-covered shelters | Per code requirements |

### Key Requirements

- Group I and II hangars typically require **foam and foam-water systems**
- Deluge systems are common for high-hazard areas
- Drainage must handle the combined flow from sprinklers, foam, and fire department operations
- Aircraft positioning and hangar door configuration affect sprinkler layout
- Supplemental protection may be required for maintenance pits and fuel dispensing areas

## Cold Storage and Refrigerated Warehouses

### Temperature-Based System Selection

| Temperature Range | Recommended System |
|------------------|--------------------|
| Above 40 degrees F | Wet pipe (conventional) |
| 32 to 40 degrees F | Dry pendant heads fed from wet system, or dry pipe |
| Below 32 degrees F | Dry pipe or double-interlock preaction |

### Unique Challenges

- **Fluid delivery time** is critical -- in large dry systems serving cold storage, water may take several minutes to reach the most remote head
- **Insulated panels** -- cold storage walls and ceilings use foam insulation panels that can contribute significant fuel load
- **In-rack sprinklers** are required for nearly all automated storage and retrieval systems (ASRS) in cold storage
- The combustible packaging (corrugated, plastic wrap, pallets) often represents the primary fuel load, not the frozen food itself
- **Nitrogen inerting** is used in some modern cold storage facilities to reduce oxygen levels and prevent fire ignition
- HPR (Highly Protected Risk) insurers typically require double-interlock preaction systems below freezing

## Food Processing

### Sanitation and Material Compatibility

- Harsh sanitation chemicals can corrode standard brass or chrome-plated sprinklers
- **Wax-coated, Teflon-coated, or stainless-steel sprinklers** may be required
- **Metal fusible-link sprinklers** are almost always used (glass bulbs can be damaged during cleaning)
- Sprinkler heads must withstand high-pressure washdown procedures

### Temperature Rating Considerations

- Rooms with ovens, fryers, or heat-generating equipment may require **200 degrees F or 286 degrees F** rated heads
- Areas subject to hot water washdown should use **200 degrees F** rated heads minimum to prevent accidental activation
- Cooler and freezer areas per cold storage requirements above

### Regulatory Overlay

- USDA and FDA facility requirements may influence sprinkler system design
- Food safety regulations may restrict materials and access to production areas
- Coordination with food plant engineers is essential during design

## Chemical and Manufacturing Plants

### Design Complexity

- Chemical storage room sprinkler criteria depend on the types of chemicals and storage arrangement
- Fire codes reference specialty standards beyond NFPA 13 for specific hazards
- Insurance carriers (FM Global, others) have additional suppression standards for chemical hazards
- **Extra Hazard Group 1 or 2** classification is common
- Flammable liquid storage areas may require foam-water systems

### Water Supply

- Robust water supplies are essential -- flow demands are high
- Dedicated fire water storage tanks and fire pumps are typical
- Multiple risers and zone protection are common in large facilities

## General Approach for Any Industry

When designing for any specialized occupancy:

1. **Identify all applicable codes and standards** -- NFPA 13 plus any occupancy-specific NFPA standards, IBC/IFC requirements, and insurance carrier requirements
2. **Classify hazards early** -- Get agreement with the AHJ and insurer before beginning detailed design
3. **Engage specialists** -- Complex occupancies benefit from fire protection engineering consultants
4. **Coordinate with operations** -- Understand the client's processes, materials, and operating conditions
5. **Plan for maintenance access** -- Systems in specialized environments are harder to inspect, test, and maintain
6. **Document everything** -- Specialized designs require clear documentation of assumptions, classifications, and design criteria
