Underground piping connects the water supply source (municipal main, water storage tank, or fire pump suction) to the building's fire sprinkler risers. This piping is governed by **NFPA 24: Standard for the Installation of Private Fire Service Mains and Their Appurtenances**.

---

## Underground Pipe Materials

### Ductile Iron Pipe (DIP)

The most commonly used material for fire protection underground piping.

**Characteristics:**
- Available in sizes from 4" to 64"
- Cement-lined interior (C-factor of 140)
- Polyethylene encasement recommended in corrosive soils
- Push-on or mechanical joint connections
- Very long service life (50-100+ years)
- Listed to AWWA C151/A21.51

**Typical cost:** $15-40 per linear foot depending on size, not including installation

### PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride)

Listed PVC is permitted for underground fire mains per NFPA 24.

**Characteristics:**
- Lighter than ductile iron (easier to handle)
- Smooth interior (C-factor of 150)
- Corrosion-resistant
- Listed to AWWA C900 or AWWA C905
- Must be UL listed for fire service
- Lower cost than ductile iron

**Limitations:**
- Cannot be used where exposed above ground (UV degradation)
- Less impact-resistant than ductile iron
- Cannot be used within 10 feet of building foundations in some jurisdictions (fire exposure concern)

### HDPE (High-Density Polyethylene)

Gaining acceptance for underground fire protection:

- Fusion-welded joints (virtually leak-free)
- Highly flexible -- can follow curved trench profiles
- Excellent corrosion resistance
- C-factor of 150
- Must be listed for fire service

### Steel Pipe

Used in some applications, particularly under buildings or through foundations:

- Schedule 40 or thicker
- Requires corrosion protection (coating, wrapping, or cathodic protection)
- Used where aboveground and underground piping transition through slabs

## Key Components

### Post Indicator Valves (PIV)

A PIV is a gate valve installed below grade with an indicator post extending above grade. The post has a window showing "OPEN" or "SHUT."

- Required on underground fire service mains to provide a visible, accessible shut-off
- Must be supervised (tamper switch connected to fire alarm)
- Located to allow isolation of sections of the underground system for maintenance
- Typical spacing: one PIV per building or per zone

### Wall Post Indicator Valves (WPIV)

Similar to a PIV but mounted through a building wall. The operator extends inside the building where it is accessible year-round.

### OS&Y Valves at Risers

Outside Screw and Yoke valves are typically installed at the base of each riser where the underground piping enters the building. They provide visible indication of valve position (stem position = open/closed).

### Check Valves

Installed to prevent backflow from the sprinkler system back into the water supply:

- Required at FDC connections
- May be required at the point of connection to the public main
- Backflow preventers (DCDA or RPBA) may be required by the water utility

### Backflow Preventers

Many water utilities require backflow prevention on fire service connections:

| Type | Abbreviation | Pressure Loss | Application |
|------|-------------|--------------|-------------|
| Double Check Detector Assembly | DCDA | 5-10 psi | Standard fire service connections |
| Reduced Pressure Backflow Assembly | RPBA | 10-15 psi | Higher hazard connections, chemical plants |

**Critical design note:** Backflow preventer pressure losses must be included in hydraulic calculations. A 12-psi loss through an RPBA can be the difference between a system that works and one that does not.

### Fire Hydrants

Private fire hydrants on the property:

- Provide water for fire department operations
- Serve as flow test points
- Located per fire code requirements (typically within 300-500 feet of all portions of the building)
- Connected to underground mains with a minimum 6-inch lateral
- Must include a gate valve for isolation

## Installation Requirements

### Trench Specifications

- **Minimum cover:** typically 3 to 5 feet depending on climate (must be below the frost line)
- **Bedding:** 6 inches of granular bedding below the pipe
- **Backfill:** select granular material to 12 inches above the pipe crown, then native material
- **Trench width:** pipe diameter plus 12-18 inches on each side minimum
- **Compaction:** backfill compacted in lifts per geotechnical requirements

### Thrust Restraint

When water flows through bends, tees, and dead ends, the change in flow direction creates a thrust force that can push fittings out of joint.

**Methods of thrust restraint:**

1. **Thrust blocks** -- concrete poured against undisturbed soil at fittings
   - Size calculated based on soil bearing capacity and water pressure
   - Most common method for ductile iron with push-on joints

2. **Restrained joints** -- mechanical joint fittings with restraint accessories or fused/welded joints that resist thrust forces internally
   - Required where thrust blocks cannot be poured (sandy soil, limited excavation)
   - More expensive but more reliable

3. **Tie rods and harness assemblies** -- mechanical assemblies that transfer thrust to adjacent pipe

### Clearance from Other Utilities

- **Water mains to sewer:** minimum 10 feet horizontal separation, or 18 inches vertical with water above sewer
- **Separation from structures:** varies by jurisdiction (typically 5-10 feet from building foundations)
- **Crossing utilities:** follow local utility crossing requirements

## Connection to Municipal Water Supply

### Tapping the Main

Connection to the public water main typically requires:

1. **Approval** from the water utility (water department permit)
2. **Tapping sleeve and valve** -- a mechanical fitting that allows connection to the existing main without shutting down service
3. **Tapping machine** -- specialized equipment that cuts into the main under pressure
4. **Inspection** by the water utility during the tap
5. **Backflow prevention** as required by the utility

### Metering

Some jurisdictions require a fire service meter (detector check meter) that:

- Monitors fire service water usage
- Detects unauthorized use of the fire service connection
- Has very low pressure loss (bypass arrangement around the meter)
- Must be sized to pass the full fire protection demand

## Testing

### Hydrostatic Testing

All underground piping must be hydrostatically tested before connection to the sprinkler system:

- Test pressure: **200 psi** for 2 hours (or 50 psi above the working pressure, whichever is greater)
- No leakage permitted for welded or fused joints
- Allowable leakage for mechanical joints per AWWA standards
- All thrust restraint must be in place before testing

### Flushing

Underground piping must be flushed after installation and before connection to the system:

**Minimum flushing flow rates:**

| Pipe Size | Minimum Flow (gpm) | Flow Velocity |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------|
| 4" | 390 | 10 fps |
| 6" | 880 | 10 fps |
| 8" | 1,560 | 10 fps |
| 10" | 2,440 | 10 fps |
| 12" | 3,520 | 10 fps |

- Flush until water runs clear
- Direction of flow should be from the supply toward the building
- All debris (dirt, rocks, plastic, joint lubricant) must be removed
- Document the flush (date, duration, flow rate, results)
- AHJ may witness the flush

## Flow Tests

### Purpose

Flow tests determine the available water supply capacity at a specific point in the water distribution system. Results are used in hydraulic calculations to determine whether the supply can meet the sprinkler system demand.

### Flow Test Procedure

1. **Select test location** -- as close to the proposed connection point as possible
2. **Record static pressure** (no water flowing) at a residual hydrant
3. **Flow water** from one or more hydrants while recording:
   - Residual pressure (at the residual hydrant, while flowing)
   - Flow rate (from the flow hydrant, measured with a pitot gauge)
4. **Plot the supply curve** using the N^1.85 method

### Flow Test Data

A flow test report includes:

| Data Point | Description |
|-----------|------------|
| Static pressure | Pressure with no flow (all hydrants closed) |
| Residual pressure | Pressure at the test hydrant while flowing |
| Flow (gpm) | Measured discharge from the flow hydrant(s) |
| Date and time | When the test was conducted |
| Location | Address and hydrant numbers |
| Weather | Temperature (affects pressure, especially if near freezing) |

### Flow Test Validity

- Most AHJs require flow tests less than **12 months old**
- Seasonal variations affect supply (summer irrigation demand reduces available pressure)
- New development near the project can affect supply
- Water system improvements (new mains, tanks, pumps) can improve supply
- Always request the most recent test data available

## Common Underground Issues

1. **Inadequate flushing** -- debris left in underground piping migrates into the sprinkler system, clogging heads and obstructing valves.

2. **Frost damage** -- piping installed above the frost line can freeze and rupture. Verify frost depth for the project location.

3. **Corrosion** -- ductile iron in corrosive soils degrades without polyethylene encasement or other protection.

4. **Thrust block failures** -- undersized thrust blocks or blocks poured against disturbed soil can fail under pressure, blowing joints apart.

5. **Missing backflow preventer in calculations** -- forgetting to include the pressure loss of the backflow preventer is one of the most common hydraulic calculation errors.

6. **Insufficient cover** -- piping too close to the surface is vulnerable to traffic loads, frost, and accidental damage during future excavation.
