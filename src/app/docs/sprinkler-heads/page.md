Sprinkler heads are the business end of the fire protection system -- they detect heat, activate, and distribute water over the fire. Selecting the correct head type, K-factor, temperature rating, and orientation for each application is a fundamental design task.

---

## Head Orientations

### Pendent

The most common sprinkler orientation. Pendent heads hang down from the piping and spray water downward in a circular pattern.

**Applications:**
- Standard for finished ceilings (offices, retail, hotels, hospitals)
- Available in standard, recessed, concealed, and flush configurations
- Most commonly specified head type overall

### Upright

Upright heads are installed above the pipe, pointing upward. The deflector directs water upward and then back down in an umbrella-like pattern.

**Applications:**
- Warehouses and industrial spaces without finished ceilings
- Areas where pendant heads would be subject to physical damage
- Above-ceiling installations in concealed spaces
- Pipe is visible and runs below the sprinkler deflector

### Sidewall

Sidewall heads mount on a wall and project water outward in a half-circle pattern, covering the area in front of the wall.

**Applications:**
- Narrow corridors and hallways
- Small rooms where ceiling-mounted heads are impractical
- Historical buildings where ceiling penetrations must be minimized
- Hotel rooms and residential spaces
- Areas with limited ceiling access

### Concealed

Concealed heads are pendent heads with a decorative cover plate that hides the sprinkler from view. The cover plate is held in place by a heat-sensitive solder or adhesive that releases at approximately 20 degrees F below the sprinkler's activation temperature.

**Applications:**
- Finished spaces requiring aesthetic appearance (lobbies, conference rooms, restaurants)
- Healthcare facilities (institutional covers for tamper resistance)
- High-end residential
- Any space where exposed sprinkler heads are undesirable

**Important note:** Cover plates are temperature-specific and finish-specific. They must match the listed sprinkler model and cannot be interchanged between different brands or models.

## K-Factors

The K-factor determines how much water flows from a sprinkler at a given pressure. The relationship is:

**Q = K x (square root of P)**

Where Q is flow in gpm and P is pressure in psi.

### Common K-Factors

| K-Factor | Orifice Size | Typical Application |
|----------|-------------|---------------------|
| K-2.8 | 3/8" | Residential sprinklers (NFPA 13D/13R) |
| K-4.2 | 7/16" | Residential sprinklers |
| K-5.6 | 1/2" | Standard spray, most common for light and ordinary hazard |
| K-8.0 | 3/4" | Extended coverage, ordinary/extra hazard |
| K-11.2 | -- | CMSA minimum, storage applications |
| K-14.0 | -- | ESFR storage sprinklers |
| K-16.8 | -- | ESFR storage sprinklers |
| K-22.4 | -- | ESFR large-drop storage |
| K-25.2 | -- | ESFR high-challenge storage |

### K-Factor Selection

- **Lower K-factors** (K-5.6) deliver less water at the same pressure -- suitable for light hazard applications where less water is needed
- **Higher K-factors** (K-8.0 and above) deliver more water at the same pressure -- required for higher hazard applications, but the larger orifice means the minimum operating pressure may produce more flow than needed for light hazards
- **Extended coverage heads** (K-8.0, K-11.2) cover larger areas per head, reducing the total number of heads needed. This can lower installation cost despite the higher cost per head
- **ESFR/CMSA heads** (K-14.0 and above) are designed specifically for storage applications with high flow demands

## Temperature Ratings

Sprinkler heads are rated to activate at specific temperatures. The rating must be appropriate for the maximum ambient temperature at the ceiling.

### Standard Temperature Ratings

| Classification | Temperature Rating | Color Code | Typical Ambient Max |
|---------------|-------------------|------------|-------------------|
| Ordinary | 135-170 degrees F | Uncolored or black | Up to 100 degrees F |
| Intermediate | 175-225 degrees F | White | 101-150 degrees F |
| High | 250-300 degrees F | Blue | 151-225 degrees F |
| Extra High | 325-375 degrees F | Red | 226-300 degrees F |
| Very Extra High | 400-475 degrees F | Green | 301-375 degrees F |
| Ultra High | 500-575 degrees F | Orange | 376-475 degrees F |
| Ultra High | 650 degrees F | Orange | 476-575 degrees F |

### Temperature Selection Guidelines

- **Standard (155 degrees F)** is the default for most occupied spaces
- **200 degrees F** should be used near heat-producing equipment (kitchens, mechanical rooms), in attics with limited ventilation, and in food processing areas subject to hot washdowns
- **286 degrees F** may be required near ovens, fryers, and high-temperature processes
- Skylights, glass roofs, and spaces near hot equipment require careful evaluation
- Using too low a temperature rating can cause nuisance activations; using too high prevents timely activation during a fire

### Glass Bulb vs. Fusible Link

**Glass bulb sprinklers** use a small glass vial filled with a heat-sensitive liquid. When the liquid expands sufficiently, the bulb shatters and the sprinkler activates.

- Color-coded liquid indicates temperature rating
- More precise activation temperature
- Susceptible to mechanical damage (impact can break the bulb)

**Fusible link sprinklers** use a metal alloy element (solder) that melts at a specific temperature, releasing the sprinkler cap.

- More resistant to mechanical damage
- Preferred in food processing (less susceptible to damage from washdown)
- Preferred in areas with vibration or impact risk

## Response Time (RTI)

The Response Time Index measures how quickly the heat-sensitive element responds to elevated temperatures.

### Categories

- **Standard response**: RTI above 80 (meter-seconds)^1/2
- **Quick response (QR)**: RTI of 50 (meter-seconds)^1/2 or less
- **Fast response (ESFR)**: RTI of 50 (meter-seconds)^1/2 or less (with ESFR-specific listing)

### When Quick Response Is Required or Preferred

- Light hazard occupancies (NFPA 13 requires QR in most light hazard applications)
- Residential sprinklers (always quick response)
- Healthcare patient care areas
- Any occupancy where early activation improves life safety outcomes

### When Standard Response Is Used

- Ordinary and extra hazard occupancies (where early activation of too few heads could deplete water supply before the fire is controlled)
- Some storage applications where ESFR heads are not used

## Extended Coverage Heads

Extended coverage (EC) heads are listed to protect larger floor areas than standard spray heads, reducing the number of heads needed.

### Advantages

- Fewer heads per floor area means lower material and labor cost
- Fewer ceiling penetrations (important in architectural applications)
- Less piping (fewer branch lines, smaller mains in some cases)

### Limitations

- Must be used within the specific spacing and area limits of their listing
- May require higher operating pressures than standard spray heads
- Obstruction rules are more critical with wider spacing
- Not available for all hazard classifications

### Common Extended Coverage Spacings

- Light hazard: up to 20 x 20 feet (400 sq ft per head)
- Ordinary hazard: up to 16 x 16 feet (256 sq ft per head) for some listings

Compare this to standard K-5.6 heads at 15 x 15 feet (225 sq ft per head maximum in light hazard). The reduction in head count can be significant in large open floor plans.

## Specialty Heads

### Dry Pendent/Dry Sidewall

Used to extend a wet pipe system into an unheated space (loading dock entry, freezer walk-in, exterior canopy). The dry barrel keeps water in the heated portion while the sprinkler deflector and seal are in the unheated space.

### Institutional Heads

Tamper-resistant heads designed for correctional facilities, psychiatric units, and similar environments where occupants might attempt to defeat the sprinkler.

### Decorative/Flush Heads

Various architectural finishes and mounting options for design-sensitive spaces. Available in custom colors, chrome, brass, white, and other finishes.

### Window Sprinklers

Designed to wet window glass to prevent fire spread through glazing. Used where fire-rated glazing is not provided between floor levels or between buildings.

## Selection Checklist

When specifying a sprinkler head, confirm:

1. **Orientation** -- pendent, upright, sidewall, or concealed
2. **K-factor** -- based on hazard and required flow/pressure
3. **Temperature rating** -- based on maximum ambient temperature
4. **Response type** -- quick response, standard response, or ESFR
5. **Finish** -- chrome, white, brass, custom (concealed cover plate finish)
6. **Thread size** -- 1/2" or 3/4" NPT (must match fitting)
7. **Listing** -- UL/cUL/FM listed for the specific application
8. **Deflector type** -- standard spray, residential, extended coverage, ESFR, CMSA
9. **Special requirements** -- corrosion-resistant coating, wax coating, dry barrel, guard
10. **Spare heads** -- NFPA 13 requires a cabinet with spare heads and a wrench on-site
