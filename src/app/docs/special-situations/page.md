Fire sprinkler design frequently encounters conditions that fall outside standard layouts. These special situations require the designer to apply code knowledge, engineering judgment, and often consult with the AHJ to develop an appropriate solution.

---

## High-Piled Storage

High-piled storage is defined as any solid-piled, palletized, rack storage, bin-box, or shelf storage **greater than 12 feet in height**. It triggers more demanding sprinkler requirements than standard storage.

### Key Considerations

- **Commodity classification** must be accurately determined (see Hazard Classification page)
- Storage height combined with commodity class determines whether ceiling-only protection is sufficient or in-rack sprinklers are needed
- **Aisle widths** affect fire development and sprinkler access
- **Flue spaces** (vertical and transverse gaps in storage) must be maintained for sprinkler water penetration
- The 2025 NFPA 13 edition uses tabular design criteria rather than density/area curves for storage applications

### Design Process

1. Determine commodity classification (product + packaging + pallet)
2. Determine storage arrangement (solid-piled, palletized, open rack, solid shelves)
3. Determine storage height and ceiling height
4. Determine aisle widths
5. Select sprinkler type (ESFR, CMSA, or CMDA with/without in-rack)
6. Apply appropriate NFPA 13 tables for the selected sprinkler approach
7. Perform hydraulic calculations
8. Verify water supply adequacy

## Rack Storage

Rack storage introduces specific challenges beyond standard high-piled storage.

### Types of Rack Storage

- **Single-row racks** -- one rack deep
- **Double-row racks** -- two racks back to back
- **Multi-row racks** -- more than two racks deep, with limited access from the sides
- **Automated storage and retrieval systems (ASRS)** -- densely packed racks served by automated equipment
- **Portable/mobile racks** -- racks on tracks that can be moved together to maximize storage density

### In-Rack Sprinkler Requirements

In-rack sprinklers may be required when:

- Storage height exceeds the ceiling sprinkler's ability to control a fire
- Commodity class is high (Class IV, Group A plastics)
- Solid shelving prevents water penetration from ceiling heads
- ASRS configurations create shielded areas

In-rack sprinkler placement considerations:

- Typically installed at every other tier, offset from ceiling heads
- Must be protected from damage by forklifts, product, and rack movement
- Require water shields to prevent premature activation from heat rising through open racks
- Piping must run through the rack structure without interfering with storage or equipment

### Flue Space Maintenance

Vertical and transverse flue spaces allow heat and products of combustion to rise and activate ceiling sprinklers. They also allow water to penetrate into the storage:

- **Longitudinal flue spaces** -- gaps between rows along the length of the aisle
- **Transverse flue spaces** -- gaps perpendicular to the aisles
- NFPA 13 specifies minimum flue space dimensions based on the storage and sprinkler configuration
- Maintaining flue spaces is an ongoing operational challenge; many warehouses struggle to keep them clear

## Mezzanines

Mezzanines with solid decks/floors create separate fire compartments that shield the space below from ceiling sprinklers.

### Sprinkler Requirements

- **Below solid-deck mezzanines**: Sprinklers are required below the mezzanine floor because the solid deck shields fires below from ceiling sprinklers
- **Below open-grate mezzanines**: Open-grate or perforated floors still obstruct water distribution from ceiling sprinklers. Sprinklers below non-solid floors compensate for the reduced effectiveness of ceiling sprinklers
- **On top of the mezzanine**: Sprinklers are required on the mezzanine level itself, designed per the occupancy classification of the mezzanine use
- **Structural support**: The mezzanine structure must support the added weight of sprinkler piping

### Design Approach

Treat the space below the mezzanine as a separate compartment:

1. Design ceiling sprinklers for the main space (above the mezzanine opening and surrounding area)
2. Design under-mezzanine sprinklers for the obstructed area
3. Design mezzanine-level sprinklers for the hazard on the mezzanine
4. Hydraulic calculations may need to consider the most demanding of these areas

## Drop Ceilings and Concealed Spaces

### When Sprinklers Are Required Above Drop Ceilings

The space between a drop ceiling and the floor or roof structure above is a concealed space. Whether sprinklers are required in this space depends on several factors:

**Sprinklers may be omitted when:**
- The concealed space is formed entirely of **noncombustible or limited-combustible materials** and has minimal combustible loading
- Where ceilings are attached to **composite wood joists** directly or using metal channels (up to 1 inch deep) with the concealed volume limited to 160 cubic feet, provided a minimum of 3.5 inches of batt insulation is installed in joist channels when metal channels are used

**Sprinklers are typically required when:**
- The concealed space contains combustible construction (wood framing, combustible insulation)
- Combustible materials are stored or routed through the space (plastic electrical conduit, communication cables in excess of code thresholds)
- The space is used for storage or any other purpose

**Note:** The NFPA 13 Technical Committee has historically had difficulty reaching consensus on exactly where to draw the line between spaces requiring sprinklers and those that do not. Each situation must be evaluated on its own merits, often in consultation with the AHJ.

### Sprinkler Types for Concealed Spaces

- **Upright heads** above the ceiling grid
- **Concealed pendant heads** mounted flush with the ceiling (for below-ceiling protection)
- **Recessed pendant heads** partially recessed into the ceiling

### Ceiling Tile Requirements

- The ceiling must be able to pass heat from a fire below to activate above-ceiling sprinklers (if used)
- Lay-in mineral fiber tiles will drop out during a fire, allowing below-ceiling sprinklers to operate
- Solid gypsum board ceilings generally require separate sprinkler protection above and below

## Attics

### General Rule

Sprinklers are **not required** in attics that are:

- Not intended for living purposes or storage
- Not containing fuel-fired equipment
- Part of a dwelling unit (combustible attic over residential)

### When Attic Sprinklers Are Required

- Attics used for storage
- Attics containing fuel-fired equipment
- Attics with combustible construction that creates a significant fire load
- When required by the AHJ based on local amendments

### Combustible Attic Considerations

In light-frame (wood) construction, attic fires can spread rapidly. Some jurisdictions and some editions of model codes have required attic sprinklers in:

- Multi-family residential buildings with combustible attic spaces
- Large-area combustible attic spaces in commercial buildings
- Buildings where fire walls do not extend through the attic to divide it into smaller compartments

## Obstructions

Obstructions interfere with sprinkler spray patterns and can prevent water from reaching the fire.

### Types of Obstructions

- **Structural members** (beams, columns, joists, trusses)
- **Ducts and mechanical equipment**
- **Light fixtures and ceiling fans**
- **Pipe and conduit**
- **Privacy curtains and partitions**
- **Signage and decorative elements**

### NFPA 13 Obstruction Rules

NFPA 13 provides detailed rules for positioning sprinklers relative to obstructions. The general principles:

- Sprinklers must be positioned to avoid obstructions blocking their spray patterns
- When obstructions are unavoidable, additional sprinklers may be needed below or beside the obstruction
- The rules differ for continuous obstructions (beams, walls) vs. discrete obstructions (columns, pipes)
- Maximum distance from sprinkler to obstruction, and minimum distance from obstruction to sprinkler, are specified based on the depth and width of the obstruction

## Historical and Existing Buildings

### Retrofit Challenges

Installing sprinklers in existing and historical buildings presents unique challenges:

- **Concealed piping** -- pipes must often be routed through walls, above ceilings, or in chases to minimize visual impact
- **Structural limitations** -- older buildings may not support the weight of water-filled pipe without reinforcement
- **Aesthetics** -- historical preservation boards may restrict visible components
- **Access** -- limited space for running mains and branch lines
- **Water supply** -- older municipal systems may have lower pressures and flows
- **Existing hazards** -- asbestos, lead paint, or other materials may be encountered during installation

### Design Approaches for Historical Buildings

- **Concealed heads** with cover plates matching ceiling finishes
- **Sidewall heads** to avoid ceiling penetrations
- **CPVC piping** where permitted, for lighter weight and easier routing in tight spaces
- **Extended coverage heads** to reduce the number of heads (and penetrations) needed
- **Stand-alone risers** in closets or utility spaces to minimize visible piping
- **Careful coordination** with historical preservation consultants

### Code Provisions

Most building codes provide some flexibility for existing buildings:

- NFPA 13 allows the pipe schedule method for modifications to existing pipe schedule systems
- The International Existing Building Code (IEBC) provides alternative compliance paths
- Many jurisdictions have adopted provisions allowing reduced requirements for historical buildings provided alternative protection measures are in place

## Freeze Protection Strategies

For areas subject to freezing where wet pipe systems cannot be used:

### Available Methods

1. **Dry pipe systems** -- most common for freeze-prone areas
2. **Preaction systems** -- for areas requiring both freeze and accidental discharge protection
3. **Antifreeze loops** -- small sections of wet piping filled with listed antifreeze solution, isolated by a check valve
4. **Heat tracing** -- electric heating cables wrapped around pipes to prevent freezing
5. **Insulation** -- pipe insulation to slow heat loss (supplemental measure, not standalone)
6. **Maintaining heat** -- keeping the space above 40 degrees F (preferred when possible)
7. **Dry pendant heads** -- individual dry heads fed from a wet system through the ceiling/wall into an unheated space

### Antifreeze System Limitations

- Antifreeze solutions must be **UL listed** and meet NFPA concentration requirements
- Annual testing is required before freezing weather to verify protection temperature
- Improper mixtures can freeze or create fire hazards
- The industry has largely shifted away from antifreeze in favor of dry systems due to safety concerns
- Antifreeze systems are limited in size and application

### Dry Pipe System Cold-Weather Maintenance

- **Low-point drains** must be drained after each operation, before freezing weather, and periodically during cold months
- **Auxiliary drains** require regular attention -- frequency depends on condensation levels
- **Dry pipe valve enclosures** must maintain minimum 40 degrees F
- Daily temperature verification of valve enclosures during cold weather
- Nitrogen fill is preferred over compressed air to reduce corrosion
