Pipe and fittings represent the largest material cost in most fire sprinkler systems. Understanding the available options, their applications, and their relative costs is essential for both designers and estimators.

---

## Pipe Materials

### Black Steel Pipe

Black steel (carbon steel) is the dominant pipe material in commercial and industrial fire sprinkler systems. It is durable, fire-resistant, and readily available.

**Standards:**
- ASTM A53 -- welded and seamless pipe
- ASTM A135 -- electric-resistance-welded pipe
- ASTM A795 -- pipe specifically manufactured for fire protection (welded, with or without galvanization)

**Advantages:**
- High strength and impact resistance
- Fire-resistant (will not burn or melt in a fire)
- Long service life in properly maintained systems
- Available in all required sizes and schedules
- Compatible with all joining methods

**Disadvantages:**
- Heavier than alternatives (CPVC, copper)
- Subject to internal corrosion, especially in dry and preaction systems
- Requires corrosion protection (painting or other coating) when exposed

### Galvanized Steel Pipe

Galvanized pipe has a zinc coating that resists corrosion. It is used in wet systems where corrosion is a concern, particularly where the pipe is exposed to moisture or corrosive atmospheres.

**Note:** Galvanized pipe should not be used in dry systems. The zinc coating accelerates corrosion at the air-water interface that occurs in dry systems, leading to premature failure.

### CPVC (Chlorinated Polyvinyl Chloride)

CPVC is a thermoplastic pipe approved for use in light hazard occupancies per NFPA 13.

**Advantages:**
- Lightweight -- significantly lighter than steel
- Corrosion-free -- no internal corrosion concerns
- Lower material cost than steel for small sizes
- Faster installation (solvent cement joints)
- Smooth interior (C-factor of 150) reduces friction losses
- Common in residential (NFPA 13D) and light hazard systems

**Disadvantages:**
- Limited to light hazard occupancies (with some exceptions)
- Cannot be used in plenums or exposed to certain chemicals
- Lower impact resistance than steel
- Temperature limitations (not for use in areas above 150 degrees F)
- Not permitted in all jurisdictions
- Requires listed fittings specific to CPVC fire sprinkler applications
- Seismic performance differs from steel

**Cost:** Approximately $1.05 to $1.92 per linear foot for material. Installed cost (including hangers, supports, fittings) is typically $8 to $12 per foot for residential applications.

### Copper

Copper is used in some fire sprinkler applications, particularly in residential and light commercial settings.

**Advantages:**
- Lightweight
- Corrosion-resistant
- High C-factor (150)
- Aesthetically acceptable when exposed

**Disadvantages:**
- Higher material cost
- Lower availability for fire sprinkler fittings
- Susceptible to theft

## Pipe Schedules

### Schedule 40

The most widely used schedule in commercial and industrial fire sprinkler systems.

**Characteristics:**
- Thicker wall than Schedule 10
- Required when using **threaded fittings** on pipe 8 inches and smaller
- Superior compression resistance and durability
- Better corrosion life due to thicker wall
- Higher weight and cost per foot than Schedule 10

### Schedule 10

A thinner-wall pipe that offers significant cost and weight savings.

**Characteristics:**
- Approximately 40-50% lighter than Schedule 40 (varies by size)
- Lower material cost per foot
- Permitted for use with **grooved fittings** (not threaded) in wet, dry, preaction, and deluge systems
- UL/ULC and FM listed for use in fire protection at up to 300 psi
- More susceptible to corrosion damage due to thinner wall
- Cannot be threaded -- joining must be by grooved, welded, or other mechanical methods

### When to Use Each

| Criterion | Schedule 10 | Schedule 40 |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Joining method | Grooved, welded | Threaded, grooved, welded |
| Pipe sizes typically used | 2" and larger | All sizes |
| Weight | Lighter | Heavier |
| Cost | Lower | Higher |
| Corrosion tolerance | Less | More |
| Common application | Branch and cross mains with grooved joints | All piping, especially threaded connections |

**Rule of thumb:** If the pipe will be joined with grooved fittings, Schedule 10 is usually the more economical choice. If threaded connections are needed, Schedule 40 is required.

## Fittings

### Threaded Fittings

Used primarily on pipe sizes **2 inches and smaller** (though can be used on larger sizes with Schedule 40 pipe).

**Common types:**
- Elbows (90-degree, 45-degree)
- Tees
- Reducers
- Crosses
- Unions
- Nipples
- Couplings

**Advantages:** Familiar, widely available, reliable

**Disadvantages:** Labor-intensive to install (pipe must be threaded), more friction loss than grooved (longer equivalent lengths for some fittings)

### Grooved Fittings

Used primarily on pipe sizes **2 inches and larger** and are the dominant joining method for fire sprinkler mains.

**How they work:** A groove is formed on the pipe end (by roll grooving or cut grooving). A rubber gasket sits over the pipe ends, and a two-piece metal coupling housing encases the gasket and is secured with bolts.

**Common types:**
- Rigid couplings (no movement)
- Flexible couplings (allow angular deflection and axial movement -- used at seismic connections, thermal expansion, and riser connections)
- Mechanical tees (for branch line takeoffs from mains)
- Grooved elbows, reducers, crosses, caps

**Advantages:**
- Fast installation (no threading or welding required)
- Allows Schedule 10 pipe
- Flexible couplings accommodate building movement
- System can be disassembled for modifications
- No hot work (welding/brazing) required

**Disadvantages:**
- Higher fitting cost per piece compared to threaded fittings
- Requires roll or cut grooving equipment
- Gaskets must be compatible with the fluid (standard EPDM for water)

**Cost range:** Grooved couplings typically range from $15 to $55+ per piece depending on size. Mechanical tees are generally more expensive. U-bolt mechanical tees can provide cost savings over standard mechanical tees.

### Welded Fittings

Used in some fabrication applications, particularly for:

- Large-diameter mains (6 inches and above)
- Branch connections (welded outlet fittings)
- Shop-fabricated spool pieces

**Considerations:**
- Requires certified welders and hot work permits
- AWS D10.9 or other applicable welding standards
- Welds must be inspected per NFPA 13
- Less common than grooved in modern installations

### Fittings Cost Considerations

Fittings typically represent 15-25% of the total material cost. Key factors:

- Grooved fittings cost more per piece but allow cheaper Schedule 10 pipe
- Threaded fittings cost less per piece but require more expensive Schedule 40 pipe and threading labor
- The net cost often favors grooved for pipe sizes 2 inches and larger
- Specialty fittings (flexible couplings, seismic-rated components) carry premium pricing
- Listed vs. non-listed fittings -- all fire sprinkler fittings must be listed

## Material Selection Guidelines

1. **Default to black steel** for commercial and industrial systems -- it is the industry standard for good reasons
2. **Use Schedule 10 with grooved fittings** for mains and larger branch lines to save cost and weight
3. **Use Schedule 40 with threaded fittings** for branch lines 2 inches and smaller, or wherever threaded connections are preferred
4. **Consider CPVC** for light hazard occupancies (offices, residential, hotels) where permitted by the AHJ
5. **Use galvanized pipe** only in wet systems where corrosion is a specific concern
6. **Never use galvanized in dry systems** -- it accelerates corrosion
7. **Specify nitrogen fill** for dry and preaction systems to reduce internal corrosion regardless of pipe material
